Understanding Cointegration & VAR Models

How carbon prices, clean energy, and fossil fuel stocks are linked — and how shocks transmit between markets.

Cointegration: Walking Together

Two drunk friends leave a pub. They stumble randomly (like stock prices), but they're holding hands — so they can't drift too far apart. That's cointegration.

Correlated ≠ Cointegrated

Correlation means they move similarly day-to-day. Cointegration means they share a long-run equilibrium — even if they wander apart temporarily, they always come back together.

Why It Matters

If carbon prices and energy stocks are cointegrated, carbon policy permanently affects stock valuations — not just temporarily.

VAR: Everything Affects Everything

A Vector Autoregression (VAR) model says: today's carbon price depends on yesterday's carbon price AND yesterday's stock prices. And today's stock prices depend on yesterday's carbon prices too. Everything is interconnected.

Granger Causality

Does past carbon data help predict future stock returns? If yes, carbon "Granger-causes" stocks. It's not true causation, but it shows predictive power.

Impulse Response Functions (IRFs)

If carbon prices suddenly spike today, what happens to clean energy stocks over the next 30 days? IRFs trace out this response path — showing how shocks transmit between markets over time.
